Background

Malaria remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in Sierra Leone, with the entire population at risk of infection. The National Malaria Control Programme (NMCP) under the Ministry of Health and Sanitation has prioritized evidence-based planning through strengthened routine surveillance via DHIS2. Understanding spatial and temporal patterns of confirmed malaria cases at the district and chiefdom levels is essential for targeted intervention deployment, resource allocation, and progress monitoring toward national malaria control goals.

Objectives

This study aims to:

  • Describe temporal trends of confirmed malaria cases in Sierra Leone over a 5-year period (2019‚Äì2023)
  • Map the spatial distribution of confirmed malaria incidence across all 16 districts and 190 chiefdoms
  • Identify high-burden districts and chiefdom-level malaria hotspots
  • Assess seasonal patterns and their variation by geographic region
  • Provide evidence to support targeted intervention planning by the NMCP

Methods

A retrospective descriptive analysis was conducted using routine malaria surveillance data from the District Health Information System 2 (DHIS2) for the period 2019‚Äì2023.

  • Data Source: DHIS2 ‚Äì Monthly confirmed malaria cases (RDT + microscopy) from all public health facilities
  • Population Data: Statistics Sierra Leone mid-year population estimates
  • Spatial Analysis: Incidence rates per 1,000 population mapped at district and chiefdom levels using QGIS/R
  • Temporal Analysis: Time-series decomposition for trend and seasonality assessment
  • Hotspot Analysis: Getis-Ord Gi* statistic to identify statistically significant clusters
  • Software: R v4.3, QGIS 3.x, DHIS2 Analytics

Key Findings

1

Confirmed malaria cases showed a rising trend from 2021‚Äì2023 after a COVID-19-related dip in 2020, with a cumulative increase of ~20% over the study period.

2

Eastern and Southern provinces (Kenema, Bo, Kailahun) consistently recorded the highest malaria incidence rates across all years.

3

Chiefdom-level analysis revealed persistent hotspot clusters in 23 chiefdoms, primarily in rural areas with limited health facility access.

4

Seasonal peaks (Aug‚ÄìOct) account for ~45% of annual cases, supporting targeted SMC implementation in eligible areas.

5

Western Area Urban showed the lowest incidence, reflecting urbanization effects and better healthcare access.

Conclusions & Recommendations

Subnational analysis at district and chiefdom levels reveals significant heterogeneity in malaria burden across Sierra Leone. Targeted interventions should prioritize the identified hotspot chiefdoms with enhanced vector control and case management during peak transmission months.

  • Strengthen chiefdom-level surveillance through DHIS2 data quality improvements
  • Deploy targeted IRS and LLIN distribution to persistent hotspot chiefdoms
  • Expand SMC coverage based on seasonal eligibility mapping
  • Integrate geospatial analytics into routine NMCP program review
